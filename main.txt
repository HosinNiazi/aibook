Leo was the caretaker of the old lighthouse. Every evening, he would climb the winding stairs and light the great lamp, a ritual he had performed for forty years.

One afternoon, a young boy from the village appeared at his door.

"Mister, why do you light the lamp every night when the moon is so bright?" the boy asked.

Leo smiled. He led the boy outside and pointed to the distant sea. "Do you see that tiny fishing boat?" he asked.

The boy squinted. "Barely."

"Now," said Leo, "do you see the small, dark shape of the rocks just beyond it?"

The boy looked harder. "No, sir. I can't see the rocks at all."

Leo nodded. "That's why I light the lamp. The moon is for those who can see the shore. The light is for those who are trying to find their way past the danger."

The boy was quiet for a moment. Then he said, "But the fishermen have maps. They know the rocks are there."

"Knowing the rocks are there," Leo said gently, "is not the same as seeing the light that tells you you've safely passed them."

The boy looked at the lighthouse, then back at the distant boat. He seemed to understand.

That night, as Leo lit the lamp, he thought of the boy's question. After forty years, it was the first time someone had asked him *why*. And in answering, he remembered the reason he had started: to be a small, steady point of hope in the vast, uncertain dark.
